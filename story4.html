Interruption in Soft Echo

This story is part of the DLEIF transmission.

The first time it happened, Nico was sitting in a café on Bleecker, sipping an overpriced cortado and trying to translate something vague but urgent into his laptop. The cursor blinked. The air shifted.

A shadow crossed the table.

He looked up.

“You again,” the man said—not loud, but with a strange certainty—before tackling him cleanly to the floor.

Silverware scattered. A cup broke. No one screamed. They just watched.

Then the man was gone.

Nico lay there, stunned, staring at the foam exposed under a torn chair cushion. The barista stepped over him to sweep up ceramic. Nobody said anything. Nobody helped him up.

The second time, it was a movie theater in Chelsea. Nico was halfway through his popcorn when the man burst in from the emergency exit and knocked him sideways from his seat.

“You again,” he said. Calm. Familiar. And vanished down the aisle.

Nico sat in the aisle for ten minutes. The usher offered him a free soda. He declined.

It kept happening. On the subway. In a bookstore. At a crosswalk.

Always the same man. Always the same words. Always gone before the question.

He stopped talking about it. People either didn’t believe him, or—worse—they did, and started leaving space around him.

Then Sarai called.

“Come outside. I’m in the car. We need to talk.”

He slid into the passenger seat, rain soaking his collar, the inside of the car warm and too quiet. She didn’t look at him, hands clenched on the wheel.

“You didn’t tell me,” she said. “About the man.”

Nico blinked. “What man?”

She turned to him, eyes already full of it.

“The one who keeps tackling you.”

He stared. “How do you know?”

“Because they’ve done this before. With others. And now he’s here for you.”

She pulled away from the curb, and the city slipped past, rain streaking the windshield like unread pages.

They parked outside an unmarked brownstone. A brass plaque beside the door read:
Tackle Shop

Nico squinted. “Is this a joke?”

Sarai didn’t answer. She just rang the bell.

A receptionist opened the door. Her nametag read: Marla, Probably.

“He’s ready,” Sarai said.

Marla nodded and led them down a narrow hallway that felt longer than it should have been—walls lined with books, coils of string, old diagrams that looked like musical scores written in electricity.

In a small room that smelled faintly of candle wax and static, a man in a kimono stood at a chalkboard. There were spirals drawn across it. And circles. And what might have been a map of silence.

“Welcome,” the man said, like they’d just arrived on time for a quiet thunderstorm. “You’ve been flagged.”

Nico blinked. “Flagged?”

The man nodded, gently. “By your higher field.”

“...What does that mean?”

The man turned to the board. Drew a circle. Then a bolt through it.

“It means part of you is trying to get your attention,” he said. “And he’s not subtle.”

“The tackling?”

“Not an attack,” the man said softly. “An interruption.”

Nico looked at Sarai. She met his gaze, hand resting on his arm now.

“I know it sounds impossible,” she said. “But haven’t you always expected something exactly this strange to happen?”

Nico laughed, quietly. Then louder. Then quieter again.

And something in him went still.

Not rage. Not confusion.

It was that pressure. The kind he’d worn so long it felt like posture. Deadlines. Dumbbells. Calendars full to the margins. A voice always whispering: Earn your air.

From behind a curtain: footsteps.

Measured. Heavy. Familiar.

The man stepped forward—boots first. Worn. Certain.

Nico inhaled. It was him. Older. Not by years, but by weight. The version that had dropped the pretense.

He wasn’t sure if he was about to strike or break. But the charge in his body—the tension—leaked away like warmth from a cracked cup.

“Finally,” the man said.

Nico looked at him. “Who are you?”

The man smiled, not as answer, but as welcome.

“I’m who you become,” he said, “when you stop explaining everything.”

Nico didn’t speak. He just exhaled. A long one. Felt the tension slide from his hands, his jaw, his history.

“You again,” he whispered.

And this time—it didn’t hurt.
